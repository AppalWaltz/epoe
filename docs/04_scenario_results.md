## 2. Reading each budget like an adult

**A. Growth & Capital Optimization** (α: growth 2.0, distortion 2.0, health 0.4, equity 0.2; tax increases off the table by political premise). The optimizer does exactly what its coalition says: across-the-board spending restraint — Social Security −3%, Medicare −5%, Medicaid −7%, every nondefense discretionary line −15 to −30%, *and defense −12.8%, riding down to its 2.5%-of-GDP industrial floor* — no new revenue, deficit to 4.2% of GDP. The annotation that matters: even with maximal restraint inside statutory one-cycle bounds, **debt/GDP in 2036 is still 107%** — spending-side-only consolidation, at politically survivable speeds, slows the debt ratio's climb but does not reverse it, because the interest term now compounds faster than feasible cuts accumulate. Its first-year GDP effect is −$425B (fiscal drag), partially offset over the decade by the lower debt path. *Confidence: the direction is HIGH; the −5.8 growth-utility score depends on multipliers (LOW) and overstates pain if cuts fall on low-multiplier lines.*

**B. Social Welfare Maximization** (α: health 2.0, equity 2.0). Tax-financed expansion: effective individual rate +1.5pts (+$433B), corporate to 28% (+$109B), full capital-income package (+$120B), payroll cap lifted (+$180B), carbon at $80/t (+$166B) — roughly **+$1.06T of new annual revenue** funding Medicaid +25%, ACA subsidies +60%, income security +30%, public-health/NIH +60%, education +50%, science +50%, housing +45%, plus Social Security +6.7% and Medicare +4.2%. Deficit *falls* to 4.9% of GDP even with $800B of new program spending, because the coalition's own constraint (deficit no worse than baseline) binds. Notice what the optimizer did **not** do: it did not cut defense below baseline — the symbiosis floor and reallocation-capacity cap forced expansion to be revenue-financed rather than swords-to-plowshares-financed, which is the corporate–state entanglement of Document 01 doing exactly what the source document predicted it would. *Tripwire: this scenario leans hardest on the ETI and profit-shifting parameters; if real-world erosion is at the high end of the bands, ~$150–250B of this revenue does not materialize.*

**C. Nordic-Style Financing** (structural premise imposed: VAT ≥ 5%, corporate kept low). The 5% VAT raises **$540B** — the single largest instrument on the board — while corporate moves only +1pt, faithfully reproducing the actual Nordic architecture the source document analyzed: *universal services financed by broad, efficient, regressive consumption taxes on the middle class, not by squeezing mobile corporate capital.* Total new revenue +$1.4T; the spending side matches B's health/education/science expansion and adds transportation +40%. The result is the **highest health utility (+6.3) with a stabilized debt ratio (101% in 2036)** — but equity (+5.3) is *half* of scenario B's, because the VAT's regressivity claws back what the programs give. That arithmetic — services-rich but financing-regressive — is the genuine Nordic trade-off, and the model surfaces it without editorializing. *Tripwire: the −0.9 VAT demand multiplier makes year-one GDP −$136B; phase-in design (not modeled) matters enormously here.*

**D. Deficit-First Grand Bargain** (α_debt = 3.0; VAT excluded; changes limited to 80% of statutory range). The hawk scenario achieves the best fiscal numbers on the board — deficit 3.5% of GDP, primary deficit down to **$73B (near primary balance)**, debt/GDP *declining* to 98% by 2036 — via broad new revenue (+$0.99T) plus cuts to low-θ lines (other mandatory −15%, general government −20%, international −30%). The empirically interesting result: **even an optimizer that cares three times more about debt than anything else protects and expands the high-MVPF programs** (Medicaid, public health, education), because cutting a program whose measured value-per-dollar exceeds one is a *false economy even on hawkish terms*. That is the Hendren–Sprung-Keyser literature speaking through the machinery, and it is this project's most policy-relevant single finding. *Caveat honestly: this coupling of "hawk" constraints with MVPF-respecting θ-weights describes a coalition that follows evidence over identity; relabel it aspirational if you prefer.*

**E. Pragmatic Compromise** (every dial limited to 60% of its statutory range — the tightest political-feasibility screen; VAT off the table). Moderate everything: individual +1.5pts, corporate ~+6.9pts, payroll cap, capital package, carbon ~$80/t; Medicaid +15%, public health +36%, education +30%, science +30%, defense **+9%** (the side-payment that makes the rest of the package passable — the model rediscovered logrolling), deficit 3.8%, debt stabilized at 101%. Its year-one GDP effect is the gentlest on the board (−$36B). This is the scenario the source document called the Pareto-frontier compromise, and its profile — *no actor's maximum, every actor's tolerable* — is what a vote-able budget arithmetic looks like.

## 3. Cross-cutting findings (the part worth a lawmaker's ten minutes)

**First**, the baseline is the radical option: doing nothing means 121% debt/GDP by 2036 with net interest growing to 4.6% of GDP — every optimized scenario, *from either ideological direction*, beats it on fiscal sustainability. **Second**, the corridor of overlap is visible: four of five scenarios converge on expanded public-health infrastructure, education, and science investment; carbon pricing at the statutory ceiling appears in every scenario allowed to use it (a Pigouvian instrument is the only "free lunch" the math finds); and payroll-cap reform appears in every scenario allowed to touch it — which also addresses the 2032 OASI exhaustion the baseline otherwise walks into. **Third**, the entanglement constraints bind in every single scenario — the defense floor, the health-administration floor, or the reallocation cap is active in all five — which is the quantitative confirmation of the source document's central thesis: *the corporate–state relationship is not a talking point; it is an active constraint surface that every politically real budget slides along.*

## 4. Sensitivity ranking and tripwires (what to watch, and the fallback)

Parameters ranked by how much their plausible range reorders conclusions: **(1) θ-weights / MVPF calibration** — the ethical core; remedy: publish results under at least two θ-vectors (egalitarian and utilitarian) when this goes public. **(2) Fiscal multipliers** — scenario A's pain and B's stimulus both shrink toward zero at the low end of Ramey's ranges. **(3) ETI and profit-shifting elasticities** — determine whether B/D's revenue is real; *watch:* quarterly corporate receipts versus CBO projection, inversion/IP-migration announcements. **(4) Interest rates** — a +100bp shock adds ≈$320B/yr to interest within a few years and tightens every scenario; *watch:* the 10-year term premium; *fallback:* the deficit caps in `run_scenarios.py` are the contingency dial — tighten them 0.5% of GDP. **(5) The tariff line ($330B, MEDIUM, litigation-exposed)** — if courts void major schedules, every scenario loses ~1% of GDP in revenue; *fallback:* re-run with `customs` at $150B; the framework absorbs it in seconds, which is the operational meaning of "successive budgets in the future." **(6) Recession risk** — the model assumes CBO's no-recession path; a standard recession adds $400–800B to the deficit for 1–2 years independent of policy; no scenario's *ranking* changes, but all levels shift.
