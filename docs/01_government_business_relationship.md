# 01 — The Government–Business Relationship, Measured

*Characterization without connotation: what the corporate–state structure actually is, established from primary sources, so that it can enter the optimization as a constraint rather than as an accusation.*

---

## 1. The premise, restated empirically

The source document for this project proposes that the federal government and large-scale private enterprise are, as of 2026, practically inseparable — and that any serious budget framework must treat that entanglement as a structural fact. The claim is testable. There are four measurable channels through which the entanglement operates, and each can be quantified from statutory disclosure systems that exist precisely because Congress required the money to be counted.

**Channel 1 — Direct purchasing.** The federal government is the largest single customer in the American economy. Federal contract obligations have run on the order of three-quarters of a trillion dollars annually in recent fiscal years (USAspending.gov; verify the current-year total there — this is the one figure in this section we cite as an order of magnitude rather than a fixed number, because award data revises continuously). Roughly half of the ~$900B defense discretionary budget flows to private contractors, with five prime contractors capturing a structurally dominant share. The practical meaning for a budget model: a large cut to the defense function is not merely a policy preference — it is a regional-employment and industrial-capacity shock with its own macroeconomic feedback. The model encodes this as a floor (the *symbiosis constraint*, §5 of Document 03), not as an opinion.

**Channel 2 — The hidden budget of tax expenditures.** The Joint Committee on Taxation estimates federal tax expenditures — statutory carve-outs that operate exactly like spending but are executed as revenue forgone — at **$2.2 trillion in FY2025 and $2.3 trillion in FY2026**, roughly **44 percent of all federal revenues**, a sum that *exceeds all discretionary spending combined* (JCT JCX-45-25; CBO, Appendix D of the 2025 Outlook). The three largest are the exclusion of pension contributions and earnings, preferential rates on capital gains and dividends, and the exclusion of employer-sponsored health insurance. Two of those three flow disproportionately to owners of capital; the third structurally welds American health insurance to employment. A budget framework that ignores tax expenditures is auditing the visible half of the ledger.

**Channel 3 — Purchased policy attention.** Reported federal lobbying expenditures set a record of **$5.08 billion in 2025** — the largest year-over-year jump in over a decade — with the health care sector alone investing a record **$868 million**, finance/insurance/real estate $711 million, and defense $191 million (OpenSecrets, January 2026). The most heavily lobbied issue area in 2025 was, precisely, **federal budget and appropriations** (5,189 organizations), and the single most-lobbied measure was the 2025 reconciliation act, with 2,354 organizations reporting activity. This is the mechanism the public-choice literature calls *concentrated benefits versus diffuse costs*: a provision worth billions to one industry justifies millions in advocacy, while the per-citizen cost of that provision never justifies any individual citizen's counter-lobbying. The result is a systematic, measurable tilt in whose preferences reach the legislative drafting table. (Classic empirical anchor: Gilens & Page, *Perspectives on Politics*, 2014, finding that average citizens' preferences have near-zero independent association with policy adoption once economic elites' and organized interests' preferences are controlled for. Treat its strong reading with care — it has serious methodological critics — but its weak reading, that organized money has disproportionate access, is not seriously disputed.)

**Channel 4 — Private administration of public programs.** Roughly half of Medicare beneficiaries are now enrolled in privately administered Medicare Advantage plans; most state Medicaid programs are delivered through private managed-care organizations; and the post-2010 individual market is, by design, a regulated private marketplace. The largest health insurers therefore derive a major share of revenue *from* the federal budget. This is the deepest form of the entanglement: public programs whose delivery infrastructure is itself a private industry, which means program redesign is simultaneously industrial policy.

## 2. What follows analytically (and what does not)

The neutral characterization is this: **the United States operates a mixed political economy in which the boundary between public budget and private revenue is porous in both directions** — public dollars constitute private markets (Channels 1 and 4), and private resources shape public allocation (Channels 2 and 3). The historically familiar labels for the mechanics are *regulatory capture* (Stigler, 1971) and *rent-seeking* (Tullock, Krueger), but the model does not need the labels; it needs the magnitudes, and the magnitudes are above.

What does *not* follow is any conclusion that the entanglement is simply parasitic. The same channels deliver genuine capabilities: a defense industrial base, an at-scale claims-processing infrastructure, an employer-anchored insurance system covering ~155 million people, and the world's deepest capital markets — which, as the source document correctly insists, remain the engine of the country's wealth. Capitalism's allocation machinery is spectacularly effective inside its domain of validity (priced, excludable, information-symmetric goods) and systematically fails outside it (externalities, public goods, and the textbook information asymmetries of health care — Arrow, *American Economic Review*, 1963). The optimization framework therefore neither dismantles the engine nor worships it; it constrains the search space to budgets the engine can survive, then maximizes well-being inside that space.

## 3. From characterization to constraint

Three model objects in Document 03 are the formal residue of this section. The **symbiosis floors** ($x_k \geq \beta_k Y$) encode Channel 1 and Channel 4: defense cannot fall below an industrial-base share of GDP in one cycle, and the major health programs cannot contract faster than their private administrative layer can absorb. The **capital-mobility erosion terms** in the revenue functions encode the disciplining force that mobile capital exerts on tax policy (the empirical profit-shifting semi-elasticity, ~0.8–1.5 percent of base per statutory point — Heckemeyer & Overesch meta-analysis). And the **political-feasibility bounds** encode Channel 3 honestly: the model does not pretend lobbying away; it narrows the per-cycle movement of each line to ranges that have historically survived the gauntlet.

One deliberate omission, flagged for intellectual honesty: the model does not *endogenize* lobbying — it does not let industries fight back inside the simulation. That is a research frontier (computational public choice), and pretending to have solved it would violate this project's epistemics. The constraint-based treatment is the defensible current state of the art.
